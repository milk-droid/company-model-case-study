The Problem

Pages tell coherent stories. The site doesn't. Hundreds of marketing pages, written by different teams over years, in many locales. The same product gets named two different ways in three different places. There's no machine-readable model of what the company says - so there's no way to author at the system level.

The Approach

Stop authoring pages. Author the system that produces them. Extract everything the site already says. Reduce it to canonical entities. Make it queryable. Then let an LLM compose new pages out of those entities.

The 4-Layer Architecture

1

Extraction

Crawl, scrape, and LLM-extract structured entities from existing pages. Turn unstructured marketing copy into machine-readable data.

2

Canonical Model

A deduplicated entity database that spans pages and locales. One source of truth for what the company says about each product, feature, and benefit.

3

Generative Content

LLM assembles page scaffolds from real entities. New pages are composed from the model, not invented from scratch.

4

Adaptive Layer

Runtime personalization based on audience and context. Planned but not yet built - the architecture makes room for it.

I designed and built layers 1-3. The boundaries between layers are the design move - drawing them clearly is what keeps the system composable.

How I Worked with AI

1
Talk to the data first

Before sketching anything, sat with Claude and wrote SQL. Wanted to know what the distributions actually looked like. Several early ideas died here, which is the cheapest place for them to die.

2
Build the dirt road first

Before any UI, shipped the pipeline as a CLI. Ugly, fast, real. Once the data shape was right, the UI was almost a transcription.

3
Plan, paste, implement

Every chunk of work began as a written plan pasted into a Claude session - context, current state, change list, success criteria.

4
Hold the line

Knowing what NOT to redesign is a design move. When the team said "keep the graph as-is," that constraint made the work better, not worse.

Design Decisions

Lead with the question, not the graph

A force-directed view is gorgeous, but users needed to answer "where do we have a gap?" - so structured views got the front page.

Make absence visible

Most heatmaps render zeros faint. Here, empty cells are outlined hard, so the gaps read as clearly as the hits.

Wait until the data was real

A generation demo built on a thin graph would have set wrong expectations. Held Layer 3 back until Layer 2 had enough volume.

Reflection

The shape of the data is the shape of the design.

The thing I'm proudest of is the seam between the layers. You can swap out the LLM in Layer 1 and Layers 2-4 don't notice. The boundaries hold because they were drawn early, on purpose.
